Coherent Corp.
The Quiet One

Everyone is talking about Lumentum. Lumentum is up 1,255% in a year. Coherent does the same thing, for the same customers, using the same technology. It's up 449%. I want to understand why that gap exists and whether it should.

I found Coherent by searching for whatever Lumentum's less famous cousin was. Turned out to be a company doing $7B in annual revenue with a $2 billion equity check from Nvidia sitting on its balance sheet and AI transceiver capacity booked through 2028. I read the Q3 earnings call twice because I thought I was missing something obvious. I don't think I was. This is my attempt to figure out if the market is pricing that correctly.

The Business

They sell light. Specifically the kind that makes AI work.

Here's the problem Coherent solves. When you train a large language model or run inference at scale, you have hundreds or thousands of Nvidia GPUs that need to talk to each other constantly. For years they did this over copper wires. Copper works fine until you're trying to move more data than copper can carry, which is exactly where we are now. The shift from electrical to optical connections inside data centers is happening in real time, and it's happening because AI made the bandwidth requirements jump faster than copper could keep up.

Coherent makes the components that enable that shift. Specifically: optical transceivers, which are modules that slot into network switches and convert electrical signals into light pulses, send them through fiber optic cables, and convert them back at the other end. The "800G" and "1.6T" numbers you see attached to transceivers refer to how many gigabits per second they can move. 1.6T means 1.6 terabits per second, which is roughly 200 gigabytes per second. Nvidia's newest GPU clusters need connections operating at these speeds.

Coherent also manufactures the indium phosphide (InP) lasers that go inside those transceivers. This is the part that matters for long-term competitive positioning. InP lasers are the core component that makes high-speed optical transceivers work, and making them at scale requires expensive fabrication facilities. Coherent is currently scaling its 6-inch InP wafer production and plans to more than quadruple capacity by end of 2027, per the Q3 FY2026 earnings call (May 6, 2026). Very few companies in the world can do this at volume. Coherent, Lumentum, and Applied Optoelectronics are the main names.

The Nvidia relationship deserves its own paragraph. In Q3 FY2026, Nvidia took a $2 billion equity stake in Coherent and signed a multi-year supply agreement covering Coherent's laser and optical networking products for AI data centers. This was disclosed in the Q3 8-K filing (SEC EDGAR, May 6, 2026). Customers don't take equity positions in their suppliers. That's not a vendor relationship. That's a strategic bet on Coherent's manufacturing capability being critical infrastructure for Nvidia's roadmap.

I genuinely did not know what an indium phosphide laser was before starting this. The short version: it's a semiconductor laser made from indium and phosphorus instead of the more common gallium arsenide. InP lasers run faster and at lower power for high-bandwidth applications, which is why the industry shifted to them for 800G and 1.6T transceivers. The 6-inch wafer scaling Coherent is doing is significant because larger wafers mean more components per production run, lower unit cost, higher margins over time. I got this from the Futurum Research Q2 and Q3 FY2026 analyses and confirmed it against the earnings call transcript.
The Thesis

Lumentum got discovered. Coherent got overlooked. I want to know if that's still true.

Lumentum's 1-year return of 1,255% is not hype. It's backed by real revenue growth. Lumentum's datacenter revenue grew roughly 90% year over year in its most recent quarter, faster than Coherent's 41%. Their story is cleaner because they divested their legacy telecom segments earlier and the datacenter number doesn't have an industrial segment dragging down the headline.

Coherent is messier. Industrial revenue fell 41% year over year in Q3 FY2026, per the 8-K, and it's still 25% of total revenue. That drags the blended growth rate from 41% (datacenter) down to 21% (total). If you glance at the top-line number without reading the segment breakdown, Coherent looks like a slower-growing company. It isn't, in the part that matters.

The valuation gap: Coherent trades at roughly 49x forward earnings. Lumentum trades at roughly 159x forward earnings. Both numbers are from JPMorgan's June 12, 2026 note via CoinCentral, which also noted that both stocks are down 15-17% from their early June highs on co-packaged optics (CPO) timeline concerns. JPMorgan reiterated Overweight on both with a $380 price target on COHR and $1,130 on LITE. The bank's channel checks suggested CPO adoption is on track, not slipping.

My read: the gap between 49x and 159x is not fully explained by Lumentum's faster datacenter growth or cleaner segment mix. Some of it is narrative momentum. Lumentum got discovered by the AI trade earlier, the stock ran, and the multiple expanded with it. Coherent is catching up. Whether it fully closes the gap depends on whether the industrial segment stabilizes and whether management gets better at telling the AI story to investors.

This is a sum-of-parts DCF on Coherent. The datacenter segment gets a full discounted cash flow valuation because it's the growth business and deserves forward-looking treatment. The industrial segment gets an EV/EBITDA multiple because it's a mature, slower-moving business and a multiple is the honest way to value it. Net debt gets subtracted at the end to get to equity value per share.

I'm going to explain every assumption below before you touch any sliders, because I think that's the only way this is actually useful. The model is only as good as the inputs. If you disagree with my assumptions, change them. That's the point.

Where the base numbers come from

Base datacenter revenue ($5.6B annualized): Q3 FY2026 datacenter revenue was $1.36B per the Coherent 8-K. I annualized that at $5.6B, which is conservative relative to Q4 guidance of $1.91-2.05B total revenue (implying datacenter around $1.4-1.5B for Q4 if the 75% mix holds). I used Q3 rather than the midpoint of Q4 guidance because I wanted to start from a number that's already happened, not projected.

Industrial EBITDA ($180M): Industrial revenue was $444M in Q3 FY2026. Non-GAAP operating margin for the industrial segment runs below corporate average. I estimated $180M annual EBITDA, which implies roughly a 10% operating margin on a $1.8B annualized run rate. This is a rough estimate. Coherent doesn't break out segment EBITDA cleanly in the filing.

Net debt ($2.7B): Coherent carried approximately $6.1B in long-term debt and $3.4B in cash and equivalents as of the most recent balance sheet data (Yahoo Finance / SEC filings). Net debt of approximately $2.7B.

Shares outstanding (~195M): Diluted share count from Q3 FY2026 earnings release. Non-GAAP diluted EPS of $1.41 on net income of approximately $276M implies roughly 195M diluted shares.

Why I think this works
Nvidia's $2B equity stake is not a normal thing. I've been trying to find another example of a hyperscaler taking an equity position in an optical component supplier and I can't find one. That's not a procurement relationship. Nvidia needs Coherent's manufacturing capability to be healthy and scaling for its own roadmap. That's a different kind of certainty than a purchase order.

The backlog gives the model real grounding. Management said on the Q3 call that AI transceiver capacity is fully booked through at least 2028, with some contracts extending to 2030. That's not a soft demand signal. The years 1-3 projections in my DCF are not speculative, they're essentially already sold.

The multiple gap versus Lumentum is too wide to ignore. Both companies are growing their datacenter segments fast, serving the same hyperscaler customers, using similar technology. Lumentum at 159x and Coherent at 49x can't both be exactly right. One is mispriced. My guess is it's the cheaper one.
What I'm not sure about
The industrial drag might last longer than I'm modeling. I've essentially ignored the industrial segment in the DCF other than valuing it on a low multiple. If industrial keeps declining, the overall company narrative stays muddy and the multiple may not expand even if datacenter executes.

Co-packaged optics (CPO) timing is genuinely uncertain. CPO is the next architectural shift, where optics get moved inside the chip package rather than plugged into a switch. If that adoption accelerates, pluggable transceiver demand could peak sooner. Both Coherent and Lumentum are investing in CPO too, but nobody has a clear answer on when it inflects.

Customer concentration is a real risk. Nvidia likely represents a very large portion of the forward revenue outlook for both Coherent and Lumentum. That's not a problem until it is. Jensen Huang's public comments move this stock meaningfully.
My honest limitations here

I don't have a Bloomberg terminal. I can't build the full CAPM from a proper beta dataset. My industrial EBITDA estimate is rough because Coherent doesn't break it out cleanly. The FCF margin progression is my own logic based on what similar companies have done as they scale, not from a financial model built off Coherent's own capex guidance. I think the directional logic is sound. The specific numbers should be treated as a framework, not a precise prediction.

If I were doing this at an actual fund I would spend a week on the cost of debt, build a real CAPM, model the capex schedule from 10-K filings, and track InP capacity utilization quarterly. I'm a student. This is the best version of that analysis I can do with public data and honest effort.

01 — Investment Thesis
Three reasons to own
FTAI right now.
FTAI has built an irreplaceable position in the CFM56 engine ecosystem — the world's most widely operated narrowbody jet engine. That asset base is now being leveraged in two distinct high-margin businesses, with a third optionality layer that the market has not fully priced.
01
The MRO Moat
FTAI's Module Factory concept redefines how airlines maintain CFM56 engines. By refurbishing and selling individual engine modules rather than whole engines, FTAI captures 30–40% cost savings vs. OEM maintenance — a structural advantage that compresses over the 20-year+ remaining life of the 12,000+ CFM56 engines still in service. Airlines and lessors are locked in.
02
Asset-Light Flywheel
The $6 billion Strategic Capital Initiative (SCI) fund closed in 2025, allowing FTAI to recycle owned assets into third-party managed capital. This transforms FTAI from a balance-sheet-heavy lessor into a fee-generating asset manager — higher ROE, lower capex intensity, and more predictable cash flow. 2026 EBITDA guidance raised to $1.6B+.
03
FTAI Power Optionality
In late 2025, FTAI announced it would convert surplus CFM56 engines into aeroderivative power turbines for AI data centers. At $500M–$1B in incremental annual EBITDA potential, this initiative is not yet in consensus estimates. Palantir partnership announced. First commercial deployments expected H2 2026.
Why now? Q4 2025 revenue of $662M missed the $702M analyst consensus, and diluted EPS of $1.08 missed estimates of $1.25. The stock sold off roughly 6–7% on the print. The underlying business — FY2025 Aero Products EBITDA up 76%, net income of $477.5M vs. a loss in FY2024, 2026 guidance raised — remains intact. This is a miss on timing, not fundamentals. Source: SignalBloom / FTAI FY2025 earnings release.
02 — Business Model
Two segments.
One moat. One option.
FTAI operates through Aviation Leasing and Aerospace Products. A third segment — FTAI Power — is nascent but carries the most asymmetric upside.
Segment 01
Aviation Leasing
Owns and manages 290 aviation assets including 47 commercial aircraft and 243 engines (as of Dec 31, 2025). The leasing book generates stable contracted cash flows, primarily from narrowbody operators flying CFM56-powered 737NG and A320ceo aircraft. FTAI's differentiation here is operational: it manages aircraft with a view toward extracting the highest parts and module value over the full asset life, rather than simply collecting rent.

Aerospace Products

◆ New Initiative · H2 2026 Launch
FTAI Power: The Wild Card
FTAI holds a surplus pool of CFM56 aircraft engines that can be converted into aeroderivative gas turbines for utility-scale power generation. In 2025, FTAI announced FTAI Power as a formal platform to pursue this opportunity, targeting AI data centers and campuses requiring reliable, fast-deployable generation. The first product — FTAI Mod-1 — is on track for delivery by Q4 2026, with planned production of 100 units in 2027 (per company guidance). FTAI also announced a multi-year strategic partnership with Palantir Technologies to deploy Palantir's AIP platform across FTAI's global maintenance footprint, targeting improved maintenance scheduling and inventory optimization. This is an operational AI deployment — not a power contracting partnership — but it reinforces FTAI's technology positioning as the business scales. FTAI Power revenue is not yet in any published guidance and represents pure optionality.

06 — Risk Factors
Know what
can go wrong.
No position is without risk. These are the factors that could impair the thesis, ranked by probability and severity. The bear case above incorporates the two highest-risk items.
HIGH

FTAI Power execution risk

The power initiative is unproven at scale. Regulatory approvals, utility interconnect timelines, and customer procurement cycles for on-site generation are all outside FTAI's core competency. A delay of 12–18 months would meaningfully compress the bull-case multiple.

HIGH

New aircraft delivery acceleration

Boeing and Airbus have multi-year backlogs, but any meaningful production ramp — particularly on the MAX and A320neo — would reduce demand for CFM56 MRO over a 3–5 year horizon. This is a long-dated risk but a real secular headwind to underwrite.

MED

Balance sheet leverage

FTAI carries approximately $3.1B in net debt. In a higher-for-longer rate environment, interest expense grows and financial flexibility narrows. The SCI fund structure reduces on-balance-sheet exposure, but the core leasing book remains capital-intensive.

MED

Management fee concentration

SCI fund performance fees depend on asset returns that FTAI does not fully control. A downturn in aviation asset values (driven by macro or oil price shock) could impair NAV and trigger investor redemptions, reducing the asset management revenue line.

LOW

PMA component certification risk

FTAI's proprietary PMA (Parts Manufacturer Approval) components are central to the Module Factory margin structure. Any FAA challenge to certifications or OEM legal action challenging PMA usage would directly impact Aerospace Products margins.

LOW

Russia-related asset impairment

FTAI has 8 aircraft and 17 engines in Russia that remain unrecovered following sanctions. These assets are written down, but any adverse legal ruling or additional exposure could create headline noise. The financial impact is contained.
